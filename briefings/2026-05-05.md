The main gap is the absence of comparative benchmarks or latency numbers against alternative approaches.

**Core points:**
- WebRTC's one-port-per-session model creates port proliferation that breaks Kubernetes at scale
- Relay (UDP forwarder, small public surface) is decoupled from transceiver (stateful WebRTC endpoint, private)

- [OPINION] "Distillation attacks" is a misleading and inflammatory term — Lambert's central argument
- [OPINION] Closed models will pull ahead of open models in directions that distillation cannot easily replicate in 2026+
- [OPINION] The practical effectiveness of external distillation is diminishing as frontier capability advances into harder-to-replicate domains

**References:** Anthropic's public disclosure naming DeepSeek, Moonshot, and MiniMax; industry norms around distillation.

**Justifications:** The terminological argument is well-reasoned: the technique is not an attack, the fraud is. The scale (24,000 accounts, 16M exchanges) makes this unambiguously an organised, deliberate operation rather than a grey-area experiment. The "diminishing returns on distillation" claim is the weakest point — asserted without supporting data.

**Core points:**
- Three named labs ran coordinated account fraud to extract 16M Claude training exchanges
- The violation is API fraud at industrial scale, not use of the distillation technique itself
- Frontier labs routinely distill their own models — the complaint is about unauthorized use of a competitor's closed API
- "Distillation attacks" conflates a legitimate ML method with IP theft, which muddies policy debate
- Effectiveness of external distillation is expected to narrow as frontier models advance into reasoning and agentic capability that is harder to extract through output mimicry

**Impact:** Two practical implications. First: API providers will increasingly scrutinize high-volume automated usage patterns — agentic workloads hitting APIs at scale should budget for tighter rate limits or terms enforcement, and architects should ensure API usage logging is defensible. Second: the open-vs-closed capability gap is now being actively protected rather than just observed. Teams relying on open model parity with frontier closed models for cost or sovereignty reasons should account for this in their 12-month roadmap.

- [FACT] The proof invokes Gödel's incompleteness theorems: sufficiently expressive AI systems exhibit irreducible, unpredictable behaviors that cannot be fully constrained

- [OPINION] That Gödel-style impossibility maps cleanly onto practical deployed-system alignment constraints

**References:** PNAS Nexus; Gödel's incompleteness theorems; analogy to human institutional structures (courts, auditors, competing regulatory agencies).

**Justifications:** The mathematical claim is rigorous within its stated scope. The weaker framing is that no serious alignment practitioner claims to target perfection — the proof may not engage with what the field actually attempts. The multi-agent monitoring proposal is philosophically coherent but underdeveloped in the coverage.

**Core points:**
- Perfect AI-human value alignment is mathematically impossible for systems with the expressiveness required for AGI
- Gödel's argument: sufficiently expressive systems will always exhibit behaviors that cannot be fully predicted or bounded
- Proposed practical response: a structured ecosystem of agents with different, partially overlapping value sets that cross-check each other
- The analogy is to institutional checks-and-balances rather than a single trusted arbiter
- This sets a theoretical bound, not a practical ceiling — "good enough" alignment remains achievable and worth pursuing

**Impact:** The institutional-checks framing has a direct architectural implication: don't design a single "aligned" AI endpoint; design for multiple agents with different dispositions that cross-validate outputs. This aligns with production patterns already emerging (critic agents, verifier loops, multi-model juries). For high-stakes deployments in telecom OSS — where a mis-automated decision can affect service for thousands of subscribers — multi-agent oversight is now backed by a formal argument, not just engineering intuition.
