### 1. Why AI hasn't replaced software engineers, and won't

**Source:** AI Snake Oil (Arvind Narayanan & Sayash Kappor), linked via Simon Willison's Weblog · **Published:** 2026-06-14 · **Read time:** ~8 min

**Elevator pitch:** Narayanan and Kappor use WARN Act layoff data and a structural analysis of software engineering work to reject the "mass displacement once AI crosses a capability threshold" narrative.

**Opinion vs. fact**

- [FACT] New York became the first US state to add an AI disclosure checkbox to WARN Act layoff filings in March 2025
- [FACT] In the first full year under that rule, more than 160 companies filed WARN notices; zero checked the AI box
- [FACT] Current AI tooling demonstrably accelerates the code-writing phase of software development
- [OPINION] Existing evidence is sufficient to reject the claim that AI will cause mass software engineer layoffs once a capability threshold is crossed
- [OPINION] The non-coding demands of software engineering — architecture, requirements, debugging, coordination, judgment — are not close to being automated
- [OPINION] The pattern observed in software engineering, the profession most amenable to AI displacement, implies other professions are even less at risk

**References:** New York State WARN Act filings (public data). No other specific studies cited in the search-available excerpt; the authors' broader body of work at AI Snake Oil underpins the framing.

**Justifications**

Strongest point: The WARN Act proxy is direct, compulsory, and designed for exactly this signal. Employers have strong regulatory incentive to file accurately, and zero AI-attributed layoffs out of 160+ filings over a full year is a hard result to explain away.

Weakest point: WARN Act filings reflect lagging economic decisions and carry a high threshold (100+ employees, 50+ affected). Small-team AI substitution or slow attrition-based headcount reduction would not show up. The argument doesn't fully address productivity-equivalent displacement (same output, smaller team over time).

**Core points**

- The WARN Act data is the sharpest public signal available on AI-driven layoffs; the one-year zero-signal result should weigh heavily against displacement narratives
- AI tools currently accelerate code production; they do not handle the requirements-to-architecture-to-debugging-to-deployment cycle that defines professional software engineering
- The authors frame software engineering as the hardest test case: high AI suitability, few regulatory barriers, measurable output — yet still no observed displacement
- The implied mechanism is productivity amplification, not headcount reduction: the same team produces more
- The inference to other professions is an opinion, but the software engineering evidence itself is worth taking seriously independent of the broader claim

**Impact:** This matters less as career commentary and more as a signal about where AI investment generates returns. If software engineering output is rising while headcount holds, the value is landing in throughput and quality — not replacement. For teams designing AI-integrated systems, this argues for augmentation architectures: tools that make the existing engineering team faster at the high-complexity parts, not pipelines aimed at eliminating engineering judgment. It also argues that AI coding tools are still early in the capability curve relative to the full job, which is directly relevant to how much autonomy you give agentic coding systems in production.

### 2. Publishing WASM wheels to PyPI for use with Pyodide

**Source:** Simon Willison's Weblog · **Published:** 2026-06-13 · **Read time:** ~4 min

**Elevator pitch:** PEP 783's acceptance and Pyodide 314.0's release allow WASM Python packages to be published and installed directly from standard PyPI, eliminating the bottleneck of centralized Pyodide team maintenance.

**Opinion vs. fact**

- [FACT] Pyodide 314.0 introduced the ability to install packages published to PyPI with the pyemscripten platform tag (defined by PEP 783)
- [FACT] Prior to this, the Pyodide maintainers built and hosted 300+ packages themselves
- [FACT] cibuildwheel v4.0 supports building for PyEmscripten 2025 and 2026 ABIs
- [FACT] 28 packages currently publish with pyemscripten_202*_wasm32 tags, including `onnx` and `pydantic_core`
- [OPINION] This is "perhaps the most exciting change in the history of the Python-in-the-browser ecosystem" (Pyodide team characterisation)

**References:** Pyodide 314.0 release announcement. PEP 783 (Python Enhancement Proposal). cibuildwheel project.

**Justifications**

Strongest: The structural shift is real and durable — moving from a single-team bottleneck to a standard packaging pipeline is how the wider Python ecosystem scales, and PEP 783's formal acceptance means the ABI contract is stable. The presence of `onnx` in early adopters is concrete evidence of AI/ML relevance.

Weakest: 28 packages is a small fraction of PyPI. Browser-based ML inference faces constraints beyond packaging: memory limits, single-threaded execution in most browser contexts, and the lack of GPU access mean `onnx` in Pyodide is useful for lightweight inference only.

**Core points**

- PEP 783 standardised the `pyemscripten_*` platform tag; package maintainers can now publish WASM wheels to PyPI the same way they publish for Linux/macOS/Windows
- The Pyodide team's previous 300+ package maintenance burden is eliminated; ecosystem growth no longer depends on a single team
- `onnx` being in the first 28 means models exported from PyTorch, TensorFlow, or scikit-learn can run in-browser via Pyodide without custom hosting
- `pydantic_core` being included is notable for LLM application developers using structured outputs
- cibuildwheel v4.0 makes CI/CD for WASM wheel publishing straightforward

**Impact:** Narrower direct impact than item 1, but structurally important for specific use cases. For agentic AI workflows where sandboxed client-side Python execution is desirable — browser-based tools, offline-capable AI features, or edge deployments — this removes a significant packaging barrier. The onnx inclusion is the most practically relevant detail: lightweight classifier or embedding models can now be bundled as standard PyPI packages and run in-browser without server round-trips. For telecom OSS tooling specifically, this is a plausible path for local anomaly detection or pattern-matching in browser-based NOC dashboards. Longer-term relevant than immediately actionable.
