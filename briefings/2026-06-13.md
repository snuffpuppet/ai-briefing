### Anthropic shuts down Fable 5 and Mythos 5 following US government export control directive

**Source:** Anthropic / Ars Technica · **Published:** 2026-06-13 · **Read time:** ~6 min

**Elevator pitch:** Four days after its highest-capability public model launched, Anthropic was forced to disable Fable 5 and Mythos 5 for all global customers after the US Commerce Department issued an export control directive citing an alleged jailbreak as a national security threat.

**Opinion vs. fact**

- [FACT] The US Commerce Department issued an export control directive on June 13, 2026 ordering Anthropic to suspend Fable 5 and Mythos 5 access for all foreign nationals.
- [FACT] Fable 5 launched on June 9, 2026 — four days before the shutdown.
- [FACT] To ensure compliance, Anthropic disabled both models for all customers globally, not just foreign nationals.
- [FACT] The directive cites an alleged jailbreak method as the national security concern but provided no specific technical details.
- [FACT] Anthropic publicly stated it disagrees with the directive and considers it a misunderstanding.
- [FACT] Mythos 5, which uses the same underlying model as Fable 5 with safeguards lifted in certain areas, was being deployed via "Project Glasswing" in active collaboration with the US government.
- [OPINION] Anthropic's position that applying this standard across the industry would effectively halt all frontier model deployments is Anthropic's own regulatory interpretation, not a verified legal determination.

**References:** Anthropic's official statement (anthropic.com/news/fable-mythos-access); Ars Technica reporting; Simon Willison commentary. US Commerce Department is the citing authority. No independent third-party technical assessment of the alleged jailbreak was cited.

**Justifications**

Strongest point in favour of the government's concern: Fable 5 launched with conservative safeguards that route queries on sensitive topics to Opus 4.8 rather than the full Mythos-class model, triggering in fewer than 5% of sessions on average. This concedes that the unconstrained model has meaningfully elevated capability in sensitive domains. Mythos 5, with safeguards further lifted, carries what Anthropic described as "the strongest cybersecurity capabilities of any model in the world."

Weakest: the directive gave no technical specifics, making verification impossible. The structural contradiction is significant — the US government simultaneously operated Mythos 5 (same underlying model, fewer safeguards) via Project Glasswing, while citing national security concerns about the same model's capability.

**Core points**

- Fable 5 and Mythos 5 disabled for all Anthropic API and platform customers globally as of June 13, 2026.
- US Commerce Department export control directive cited an alleged Fable 5 jailbreak as a national security threat; no technical details provided.
- Anthropic pushed back, arguing a narrow jailbreak finding should not warrant a commercial recall affecting hundreds of millions of users; stated it is working to restore access.
- The government had separately deployed Mythos 5 (same underlying model, fewer safeguards) for elevated cybersecurity use via Project Glasswing.
- No restoration timeline was given; all API routes to Fable 5 and Mythos 5 are down.

**Impact**

This is an immediate production incident for any team that adopted Fable 5 in their API stack — the model was available for four days before being disabled with no stated restoration timeline. Beyond the outage itself, the event establishes that frontier model access can be interrupted unilaterally by government directive with no advance warning. For architects designing AI-integrated systems, regulatory disruption is now a distinct availability risk category alongside infrastructure failures and pricing changes. Multi-vendor API routing with active provider fallbacks is no longer a resilience nicety — it becomes a continuity requirement. The incident also signals that even commercially released, safeguarded models remain candidates for national security review, raising the compliance posture bar for any system routing sensitive queries through externally hosted frontier models. For telecom OSS contexts specifically — where network topology, access control, and capacity data are dual-use — this risk is heightened if frontier model APIs are part of, or planned for, the query path.
