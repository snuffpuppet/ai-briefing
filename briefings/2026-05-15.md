### Bliki: Interrogatory LLM

Source: Martin Fowler · Published: 2026-05-14 · Read time: ~4 min

**Elevator pitch:** A pattern where an LLM builds its own context by interviewing the user one question at a time, rather than requiring a human to write all context upfront.

**Opinion vs. fact:**
- [FACT] The common approach to complex LLM tasks requires humans to write extensive context documents — often several pages of markdown.
- [FACT] An alternative is to prompt the LLM to interview the user, asking all the questions it needs to construct appropriate context.
- [OPINION] Insisting the LLM ask only one question at a time produces more useful conversations.
- [OPINION] Using an interrogatory LLM to review a specification by interviewing a domain expert is more effective than asking a human to read the document directly, because people find reviewing harder than conversing.

**References:** None cited beyond the author's own practice and observation.

**Justifications:** Fowler presents two use cases from observed practice, not controlled experiments. The one-question constraint is a specific design decision he identifies — it keeps the session tractable and keeps the human from feeling overwhelmed. The document-review variant rests on a well-supported observation about human psychology (conversation is lower-friction than reading and annotating), but Fowler cites no empirical evidence for this. The weakest point is the absence of comparative data; the strongest is the specificity of the pattern, which makes it directly implementable.

**Core points:**
- LLMs can generate their own context by interviewing users, reducing the burden on humans to write complete upfront prompts.
- Constrain the LLM to one question at a time — this keeps sessions productive.
- Secondary use case: give the LLM a specification or design document and have it interview a subject-matter expert to surface errors, gaps, or unstated assumptions.
- The pattern is particularly useful wherever context quality determines output quality and humans are the primary context source.

**Impact:** A practically useful pattern for solutions architects designing AI-assisted workflows. Anywhere a system needs complex requirements fed in — architecture reviews, requirements validation, test coverage analysis — the interrogatory approach shifts the structuring burden from human author to LLM interviewer. In multi-step agentic workflows, an interrogatory initialization phase can replace fragile "fill in this template" prompts with a more robust conversation loop. The document-review variant applies directly to technical design review processes where getting a domain expert to read a 40-page spec is a known bottleneck.

### Your doctor's AI notetaker may be making things up, Ontario audit finds

Source: Ars Technica · Published: 2026-05-14 · Read time: ~6 min

**Elevator pitch:** Ontario's auditor general tested 20 AI medical scribe products used by ~5,000 physicians and found widespread hallucination, incorrect prescriptions, and missed clinical details — documented in a formal audit, not a research study.

**Opinion vs. fact:**
- [FACT] Ontario Auditor General Shelley Spence released a special report on AI Scribe programs used across the province's health-care sector.
- [FACT] 9 of the 20 AI scribe systems tested hallucinated — fabricating information not present in the actual consultation, including therapy referrals and blood test orders not discussed by the doctor.
- [FACT] 12 of 20 systems recorded incorrect drug information, such as a different drug than the one actually prescribed.
- [FACT] 17 of 20 systems missed key details about patients' mental health issues.
- [FACT] Approximately 5,000 Ontario physicians currently use AI scribes.
- [FACT] The auditor found the systems were "not evaluated adequately" before being introduced to the broader health-care sector.
- [OPINION] No known patient harm has resulted from AI scribe errors in Ontario to date — a claim that depends on voluntary reporting and adequate clinical review.

**References:** Ontario Auditor General Shelley Spence (report author); Ontario Health (program administrator). 20 AI scribe products evaluated.

**Justifications:** The findings are from direct testing, not surveys. Failure categories are specific and quantified: hallucinated referrals and test orders (9/20), wrong drugs (12/20), missed mental health content (17/20). The "no known harm" response from government is the weakest data point: it presupposes that clinicians reviewing AI-generated notes catch and do not document errors, which is exactly what inadequate evaluation would fail to verify. The audit itself found that evaluation frameworks were absent from the start.

**Core points:**
- 9 of 20 tested AI scribes hallucinated clinical content not present in the consultation.
- 12 of 20 recorded incorrect prescription information.
- 17 of 20 missed key patient mental health details — the highest-risk failure category.
- Ontario introduced these tools to ~5,000 physicians without adequate evaluation frameworks.
- Human-in-the-loop safeguard (doctor reviews before sign-off) is in place, but the audit doesn't assess whether clinicians have bandwidth to catch subtle errors.

**Impact:** For anyone designing AI-integrated systems that produce structured records consumed in downstream decisions: this audit documents the production failure mode at scale. The pattern — plausible but wrong output in high-stakes, low-structure content — is predictable. Mental health content is systematically missed because it tends to be indirect and contextual, exactly what current models handle worst. The lesson for architecture is not that AI scribes don't work; it is that output validation architecture, evaluation frameworks, and human-review bandwidth must be designed into the system before deployment, not treated as safeguards that can be added later. This evidence also strengthens the case for structured output formats (where errors are detectable) over free-text transcription.
